Level 6 Activity Banks

For CEFR C2 Level (Mastery) | 30–45 minutes | Click a card to read

Vocabulary Games

Master etymology, archaic idioms, cultural allusions, and extreme nuance.

1

Semantic Hair-Splitting

Goal: Distinguish micro-nuances.

Students dissect words with virtually identical definitions but entirely different cultural or pragmatic baggage.

Read more
Sets of heavily nuanced synonyms (e.g., obstinate vs. obdurate vs. pigheaded).
  1. Provide a cluster of advanced synonyms to each group.
  2. Groups must map the words on a matrix assessing formality, positivity/negativity, and common collocations.
  3. They must invent highly specific, one-sentence scenarios where ONLY one of those words would be socially acceptable to use.
  4. The class debates their choices.
At C2, there are no true synonyms. Emphasize that choosing "stubborn" over "obdurate" changes not the meaning, but the intellectual register of the speaker.
2

Register Chameleon

Goal: Flawless register adaptation.

Teams compete to instantly translate legal or medical jargon into colloquial dialect, and vice versa.

Read more
Excerpts from legal contracts, medical journals, and tabloid magazines.
  1. Display a dense C2 text (e.g., "The plaintiff asserts that the aforementioned stipulation is null and void").
  2. Give teams 60 seconds to translate it into modern street slang or a casual text message.
  3. In round 2, reverse the process: take a piece of raw slang ("He totally ghosted me and bailed on the rent") and elevate it to a formal legal petition.
C2 speakers navigate all levels of society. This proves they can understand a judge as easily as they can understand a teenager.
3

The Diplomat's Dilemma

Goal: Master doublespeak and tact.

Students must navigate delivering catastrophic or offensive news using extreme euphemisms and corporate jargon.

Read more
Cards detailing brutal truths (e.g., "Our product poisons rivers", "We are firing 10,000 people").
  1. Provide a brutal truth to a student acting as a PR Director.
  2. They must address the class (the press) and convey the message without ever using negative root words.
  3. Example: "Firing 10,000 people" becomes "Initiating a strategic workforce realignment to optimize operational synergies."
  4. The press must translate the doublespeak back into reality.
Discuss the ethics of language. At C2, students must understand how vocabulary is weaponized to obscure truth in politics and business.
4

Idiomatic Obscurity

Goal: Retrieve rare fixed phrases.

A high-speed drill focusing on archaic idioms, obscure binomials, and proverbs rarely found in textbooks.

Read more
Flashcards of C2 idioms (e.g., "beyond the pale", "kith and kin", "hoist by his own petard").
  1. Read a highly specific situation (e.g., "Someone sets a trap for you, but accidentally falls into it themselves").
  2. Students must recall the exact idiom that describes this ("Hoist by his own petard").
  3. They must immediately use the phrase in a spontaneous, modern conversational sentence to prove mastery.
Explain the origins of these phrases. C2 mastery involves knowing *why* we say things (e.g., what a "petard" actually was in medieval warfare).
5

The Neologism Forge

Goal: Morphological creativity.

Students invent new English words to describe highly specific modern phenomena using correct affixation.

Read more
A list of modern, unnamed experiences (e.g., "The feeling of phantom phone vibrations").
  1. Provide an oddly specific concept (e.g., "The anxiety of seeing someone typing a message for a long time, and then they stop").
  2. Students must use Greek/Latin roots, portmanteaus, or suffixation to invent a plausible English word for it.
  3. They must present the word, its part of speech, and use it in a sample dictionary sentence.
This proves ultimate command of English morphology. If they understand how words are built, they can deconstruct any unknown word they encounter.
6

Etymological Forensics

Goal: Deduce meaning from roots.

Students are given highly obscure academic words and must deduce their meaning by breaking down their etymology.

Read more
A list of obscure C2 words (e.g., pusillanimous, lugubrious, obfuscate).
  1. Write a word on the board that no student knows (e.g., "Somnambulist").
  2. Students must identify the roots (Somn = sleep, ambul = walk, ist = person).
  3. They declare the definition (Sleepwalker).
  4. Give points to the team that can correctly deduce the meaning of the most obscure words without a dictionary.
Teach common Latin and Greek prefixes/suffixes beforehand. This is the secret to passing the Reading sections of the Cambridge CPE exam.
7

Cultural Allusion Hunt

Goal: Understand literary references.

Students decipher everyday English phrases that originate from Shakespeare, the Bible, or mythology.

Read more
Phrases containing allusions (e.g., "A Trojan Horse", "Catch-22", "A Sisyphean task").
  1. Provide a sentence: "Fixing this code is a truly Sisyphean task."
  2. Students must explain who Sisyphus was, and therefore what the adjective means (endless, futile labor).
  3. They must then brainstorm a modern equivalent scenario where this phrase perfectly applies.
C2 is deeply tied to cultural literacy. Native speakers use these allusions constantly in journalism and literature without explaining them.
8

Lexical Constellations

Goal: Map connotative associations.

Groups build complex visual maps connecting words not by topic, but by their emotional and rhetorical weight.

Read more
Large A3 paper, colored markers.
  1. Give groups a core concept, like "Leadership."
  2. They must create a web branching into Pejorative (dictatorial, authoritarian), Ameliorative (visionary, egalitarian), and Neutral (administrative, bureaucratic) terms.
  3. They must then draw lines connecting words that are often confused but belong in different semantic fields.
This visualizes the "flavor" of words. At C2, using a slightly pejorative word by accident can completely derail a professional negotiation.

Grammar Games

Perfect extreme ellipsis, mandative subjunctives, fronting, and stylistic deviations.

1

Syntactic Acrobatics

Goal: Radical sentence restructuring.

Students apply extreme fronting and inversion to completely alter the rhetorical rhythm of a text.

Read more
Worksheets with standard, bland sentences.
  1. Provide a text: "The ancient castle stood at the top of the hill. A winding river lay below it."
  2. Students must completely invert the syntax for poetic effect: "At the top of the hill stood the ancient castle. Below it lay a winding river."
  3. They must evaluate how shifting the spatial prepositions to the front alters the reader's mental imagery.
This goes beyond grammar rules into stylistics. Teach them that fronting is used heavily in literature and journalism to establish setting instantly.
2

The Subjunctive Mandate

Goal: Command the English subjunctive.

Teams draft ultra-formal legal or corporate demands using the bare infinitive subjunctive mood.

Read more
Scenario cards requiring formal demands.
  1. Provide a scenario: "Your client's contract was breached. Demand immediate restitution."
  2. Students must write demands using mandative verbs (demand, insist, require).
  3. Example: "We demand that the defendant *cease* all operations immediately." (Not *ceases*).
  4. Award points for the correct use of the "bare infinitive" regardless of the subject or tense.
The subjunctive is dying in colloquial English, but remains absolutely mandatory in C2-level formal correspondence, law, and diplomacy.
3

Extreme Ellipsis

Goal: Eliminate syntactic redundancy.

Students surgically edit clunky texts, removing every unnecessary grammatical particle using advanced ellipsis.

Read more
Wordy, repetitive paragraphs.
  1. Provide a text: "Some people prefer coffee, and other people prefer tea."
  2. Students must reduce it using ellipsis: "Some prefer coffee; others, tea."
  3. Push them further with complex verb ellipsis: "He said he would finish the report, but he didn't (finish the report)." -> "He said he would, but didn't."
C2 English is highly economical. Native speakers omit understood words constantly. This exercise bridges the gap between sounding 'correct' and sounding 'natural'.
4

Alternate Histories

Goal: Manipulate complex conditionals.

Students rewrite historical events using intertwined mixed conditionals and inverted 'If' clauses.

Read more
Brief summaries of famous historical events (e.g., The Titanic, The Moon Landing).
  1. Provide a fact: "They didn't pack enough lifeboats, so many people died."
  2. Students must rewrite this using inverted 3rd conditionals: "Had they packed enough lifeboats, fewer people would have perished."
  3. Then, add a mixed conditional for present impact: "Were the ship to have survived, maritime laws would be vastly different today."
Dropping the 'If' and inverting the subject/auxiliary (Had I known...) instantly elevates writing from B2 to C2. Demand absolute structural perfection.
5

The Epistemic Spectrum

Goal: Express microscopic degrees of doubt.

Students rank and apply highly nuanced modal adverbs and phrases to express precise levels of certainty.

Read more
A scale from 0% (Impossible) to 100% (Certain).
  1. Provide a statement: "The economy will recover next year."
  2. Call out a percentage, e.g., "75% certainty".
  3. Students must modify the sentence: "It is highly probable that the economy will recover," or "The economy is bound to recover."
  4. For 10%: "It is highly doubtful..." or "The economy is unlikely to..."
Move beyond basic modals (might, must). C2 requires lexical hedging ("There is a distinct possibility," "It's a foregone conclusion").
6

Journalistic Distancing

Goal: Avoid libel via syntax.

Students rewrite scandalous accusations into legally safe, double-passive reporting structures.

Read more
Cards with direct accusations ("The Mayor has been taking bribes for years").
  1. Give teams an accusation. They must act as cautious journalists.
  2. They rewrite it using complex distancing: "The Mayor is alleged to have been accepting illicit payments for several years."
  3. Challenge them to use dummy 'It' subjects: "It has been widely speculated that..."
This integrates vocabulary (illicit) with supreme grammatical control (perfect continuous passive infinitives).
7

Cohesion without Markers

Goal: Master lexical referencing.

Students must write a cohesive paragraph *without* using obvious transition words like 'however' or 'therefore'.

Read more
A writing prompt.
  1. Ban the use of standard discourse markers (firstly, moreover, in conclusion).
  2. Students must write a 4-sentence paragraph arguing a point.
  3. They must connect the sentences using only *lexical cohesion* (synonyms, superordinates) and *pronoun referencing* (This paradigm, Such measures).
This is the secret to a Band 9 in IELTS or a Grade A in CPE. Over-relying on "Moreover" looks mechanical; lexical cohesion looks elegant and native.
8

The Pedantic Proofreader

Goal: Correct stylistic infelicities.

Students edit advanced texts not for grammatical errors, but for tautologies, split infinitives, and poor flow.

Read more
A text that is grammatically correct but stylistically awful.
  1. Provide a text: "The reason why he went was because of the fact that it was absolutely essential."
  2. Students must identify the tautology (redundancy) and rewrite it: "He went because it was essential."
  3. Have them hunt for 'hanging prepositions' or clumsy split infinitives, debating whether they should be 'fixed' based on modern style guides.
C2 grammar is about choices, not just rules. Discuss prescriptive vs. descriptive grammar (e.g., Winston Churchill famously mocked the rule against ending sentences with prepositions).

Conversation Games

Engage in Socratic dialogue, hostile cross-examination, and crisis diplomacy.

1

The Socratic Seminar

Goal: Probe abstract philosophies.

Students engage in a deep, unscripted exploration of paradoxical concepts, relying on continuous questioning.

Read more
Abstract texts or quotes (e.g., excerpts from Nietzsche or modern ethics).
  1. Arrange desks in a circle. Present a paradoxical premise (e.g., "Is absolute freedom ultimately restrictive?").
  2. The teacher remains entirely silent. Students must drive the conversation.
  3. They must respond to a peer's statement ONLY by asking a deeper, probing question to uncover underlying assumptions.
Provide linguistic frames for profound disagreement: "I respectfully challenge the premise of your argument," or "Isn't that inherently contradictory if we consider..."
2

Defending the Indefensible

Goal: Master rhetorical manipulation.

Students are challenged to argue for highly unpopular or absurd policies using flawless logic and persuasive rhetoric.

Read more
A list of absurd or highly controversial policies (e.g., "Voting should be restricted to the elderly").
  1. Assign a student an impossible stance to defend.
  2. They have 2 minutes to construct a speech utilizing ethos (credibility), pathos (emotion), and logos (logic).
  3. The class cross-examines them. The speaker must maintain their composure and logically defend the absurd premise without breaking character.
This trains ultimate cognitive flexibility. If they can persuade the class that a terrible idea is good using English, they have mastered the language.
3

Crisis Diplomacy

Goal: De-escalate hostile situations.

A high-pressure roleplay where students act as international negotiators trying to avert an impending disaster.

Read more
Confidential briefs outlining a geopolitical crisis with ticking deadlines.
  1. Divide the class into factions (e.g., Nation A, Nation B, The UN). Give them conflicting red lines.
  2. Set a visible 15-minute countdown timer. If no treaty is signed, a simulated 'war' breaks out.
  3. Students must negotiate using hyper-diplomatic language, veiled threats, and tactical concessions to reach a consensus.
Focus on the language of de-escalation and conditionality: "We view this maneuver as highly provocative," "We are prepared to stand down, contingent upon..."
4

The TED Talk Challenge

Goal: Deliver unstructured brilliance.

Students deliver a 5-minute, highly structured, persuasive monologue with absolutely zero preparation time.

Read more
A screen displaying 5 random, unrelated images (e.g., a clock, a forest, a microchip).
  1. A student stands up. Project a random image onto the board.
  2. They must instantly begin a "TED Talk" inspired by the image, adopting the persona of an industry thought-leader.
  3. Every 60 seconds, change the image. The speaker must seamlessly integrate the new visual into their ongoing philosophical narrative.
Assess their use of pacing, strategic pausing, and rhetorical questions. C2 fluency means never panicking when the topic shifts unexpectedly.
5

The Cross-Examination

Goal: Deflect leading questions.

A student acts as a hostile witness in a courtroom, deflecting rapid-fire questions with extreme semantic precision.

Read more
A backstory/alibi sheet for the witness.
  1. The 'witness' is given a brief outlining their alibi, including one fatal flaw.
  2. The class acts as prosecuting attorneys. They fire rapid, leading questions ("Isn't it true that you...?").
  3. The witness must evade, equivocate, and stall without perjuring themselves or saying "I don't know."
Teach evasive vocabulary: "To the best of my recollection...", "I can't speak to his motives, but...", "That's a gross mischaracterization of events."
6

The Think Tank

Goal: Synthesize interdisciplinary ideas.

A clash of experts from completely different disciplines trying to solve an unprecedented global crisis.

Read more
Role cards of wildly different professions (e.g., Quantum Physicist, Theologian, Supply Chain Manager).
  1. Present an impossible scenario: "An alien artifact has landed. It provides infinite energy but causes memory loss."
  2. Experts must debate what to do. They MUST use the jargon of their assigned profession to argue their points.
  3. They must synthesize their conflicting worldviews into one coherent white paper recommendation.
This forces students out of generic English and into highly specialized lexicons, a hallmark of C2 mastery.
7

Navigating the Taboo

Goal: Discuss sensitive topics elegantly.

Students practice broaching highly sensitive cultural, political, or personal taboos without causing offense.

Read more
Prompt cards detailing sensitive situations (e.g., addressing a colleague's severe hygiene issue, questioning a cultural tradition).
  1. Pairs receive a delicate scenario.
  2. They must roleplay the conversation, relying heavily on circumlocution, hedging, and extreme politeness markers.
  3. The class evaluates the social appropriateness of the language used—was it too blunt, or too vague to be understood?
C2 isn't just knowing the words; it's knowing when *not* to say them. Teach them to use conditionals to soften blows: "I was wondering if it might be possible..."
8

The Satirical Pitch

Goal: Employ irony and dark humor.

Pitching a completely useless or dystopian product using flawlessly persuasive, deadpan corporate jargon.

Read more
A list of terrible products (e.g., "A subscription service for air", "A transparent front door").
  1. Assign a group a dystopian or absurd product.
  2. They must prepare a 3-minute pitch to venture capitalists, using slick buzzwords, buzzwords, and manufactured urgency.
  3. They must never break character or admit the product is bad. The humor comes from the linguistic disconnect between the high-level rhetoric and the low-level idea.
Satire requires supreme command of a language's cultural tropes. Praise the use of vacuous buzzwords ("synergistic paradigm shift").

Reading Games

Deconstruct satire, analyze propaganda, and synthesize vast academic literature.

1

Deconstructing Satire

Goal: Identify linguistic irony.

Reading Swiftian satire or modern parody to pinpoint exactly how the irony is linguistically achieved.

Read more
Satirical articles (e.g., from The Onion or excerpts from Jonathan Swift).
  1. Provide a text that presents an absurd premise in a highly serious, academic tone.
  2. Students must identify the "tell"—the exact grammatical or lexical moments where the author signals that the text is not meant to be taken literally.
  3. Discuss the cultural context required to 'get' the joke.
Satire relies on mimicking formal registers. Teach students to look for hyperbole masked as objective data.
2

The Literature Review

Goal: Master academic synthesis.

Students synthesize five conflicting academic abstracts into a single, cohesive introductory paragraph.

Read more
5 complex abstracts from scientific or sociological journals on the same topic.
  1. Distribute the 5 abstracts. Students must skim them to identify the core consensus and the points of divergence.
  2. They must write a singular 150-word paragraph that encompasses the entire debate.
  3. Example structure: "While prevailing literature (Smith, 2020; Jones, 2021) suggests X, recent empirical anomalies (Chen, 2023) indicate Y..."
This is the exact skill required for a Master's or Ph.D. dissertation. Force them to use citation brackets and advanced reporting verbs (corroborates, refutes, postulates).
3

Reading the Unsaid

Goal: Decode literary minimalism.

Analyzing a minimalist short story to deduce the entire plot and emotional arc from what is explicitly omitted.

Read more
A minimalist text (e.g., Hemingway's "Hills Like White Elephants").
  1. Provide a text consisting almost entirely of vague dialogue with no descriptive tags.
  2. Students must act as psychological profilers. What are the characters *actually* talking about?
  3. They must highlight specific pronoun uses (e.g., constantly saying "it" instead of naming the problem) to prove their theories.
Introduce the "Iceberg Theory" of literature. C2 readers understand that 90% of the meaning in high-level English lies beneath the surface vocabulary.
4

The Editor's Scalpel

Goal: Reconstruct abstract philosophy.

Reordering a deeply abstract philosophical text where the only clues are subtle thematic shifts, not transition words.

Read more
A dense philosophical or critical theory essay cut into paragraphs.
  1. Remove all obvious transition markers (Firstly, In conclusion) from the text. Scramble it.
  2. Students must piece it back together by tracking the evolution of a single abstract concept (e.g., from 'existence' to 'consciousness').
  3. They must justify their order by pointing to lexical chains (words from the same semantic field that carry the idea forward).
This is the highest level of reading comprehension. It requires tracking the logical progression of an idea across paragraphs, irrespective of grammar.
5

Propaganda Forensics

Goal: Uncover linguistic manipulation.

Analyzing political speeches or PR statements to uncover loaded language, dog whistles, and logical fallacies.

Read more
Transcripts of famous political speeches or controversial press releases.
  1. Distribute the text. Instruct students to read it from the perspective of an opposing political analyst.
  2. They must circle "weasel words" (words that sound meaningful but are vague, like "many say" or "freedom").
  3. They must identify passive structures used to evade responsibility ("mistakes were made" instead of "I made a mistake").
Teach the concept of the "Dog Whistle"—language that sounds innocent to the general public but holds a specific, often divisive meaning for a targeted subgroup.
6

The Rhetorical Autopsy

Goal: Map Aristotelian appeals.

Breaking down a masterpiece of English rhetoric to map its structural use of ethos, pathos, and logos.

Read more
A classic piece of rhetoric (e.g., MLK's "I Have a Dream" or Churchill's "We Shall Fight on the Beaches").
  1. Students read the text with three different colored highlighters.
  2. They highlight Ethos (appeals to credibility/morality), Pathos (appeals to emotion), and Logos (appeals to logic/data).
  3. They analyze the rhythm: how does the author use anaphora (repetition at the start of sentences) to build momentum?
C2 mastery includes appreciating the beauty of English. Discuss how the cadence of the sentences mimics the emotional state the author wants to evoke.
7

Pastiche & Parody

Goal: Replicate iconic authorial voices.

Reading a famous author and attempting to write a new paragraph perfectly mimicking their unique syntactic style.

Read more
Distinctive literary excerpts (e.g., Charles Dickens, Ernest Hemingway, Jane Austen).
  1. Provide an excerpt. Students must deconstruct the "rules" of that author's universe (e.g., Hemingway = short, declarative, no adverbs; Dickens = sprawling, heavily modified, lists).
  2. Give them a mundane topic, like "Making a cup of coffee."
  3. They must write a paragraph about making coffee in the exact style of the assigned author.
This requires an incredibly deep understanding of syntax and vocabulary choice. It proves they don't just 'know' English, but can play with it musically.
8

The C2 Lexical Cloze

Goal: Retrieve obscure fixed collocations.

Filling in blanks in an advanced text where the missing words are highly obscure collocations or archaic prepositions.

Read more
A CPE-level gapped text (Open Cloze style).
  1. Provide a text with missing words. There is no word bank.
  2. The gaps require highly specific, inflexible C2 knowledge (e.g., "He took exception ____ her remarks" -> "to").
  3. Students work individually to fill the gaps, relying purely on their internalized "feel" for what sounds right.
At this level, they cannot rely on translation from their mother tongue. They must rely on their exposure to thousands of hours of native English input.

All Skills Review

Extreme integration, holistic mastery, and near-native simulations.

1

The Polyglot's Gauntlet

Goal: Survive ultimate trivia.

High-speed trivia involving obscure idioms, identifying regional dialects, and correcting grammatical exceptions.

Read more
A comprehensive slide deck with audio, visual, and textual puzzles.
  1. Create a multi-media quiz.
  2. Round 1: Listen to a 3-second audio clip and identify if the accent is Geordie, Cockney, or Scouse.
  3. Round 2: Read a C2 sentence with one missing preposition and write it on a whiteboard.
  4. Round 3: Rapid-fire 30-second debate on a randomly generated topic.
This is the 'final boss' of English learning. Make the questions brutally difficult. Celebrate the obscurity of the English language.
2

CPE Simulation

Goal: Master exam stamina.

Surviving the most brutal parts of the Cambridge C2 Proficiency exam under strict, unforgiving time limits.

Read more
Authentic CPE past papers.
  1. Administer the "Reading and Use of English" part of a CPE paper.
  2. Enforce absolute silence. Give them exactly the time limit allowed in the real exam.
  3. Following this, pair them up immediately for the collaborative speaking task, analyzing their ability to shift from silent focus to active engagement.
C2 exams test psychological endurance as much as language. Teach them when to guess and move on rather than obsessing over one unknown word.
3

The Treaty Drafters

Goal: Write legally binding language.

Students not only debate an international crisis, but must collaborate to write the exact legal language of a binding treaty.

Read more
Sample UN resolutions for structural reference.
  1. After concluding a Model UN debate, alliances must sit down to draft a resolution.
  2. They must use formal legal syntax: "Recognizing that...", "Hereby mandates...", "Subject to the provisions of..."
  3. Opposing alliances will read the treaty looking for legal loopholes or ambiguous phrasing to exploit.
Legal English (Legalese) requires absolute precision. A misplaced comma or the use of "may" instead of "shall" can change the entire meaning of the document.
4

The Disinformation Campaign

Goal: Reverse-engineer fake news.

Groups design a highly sophisticated linguistic hoax, then swap with peers to debunk it using critical analysis.

Read more
Laptops or paper to draft the 'hoax' articles.
  1. Groups invent a plausible but fake news story (e.g., "New study shows coffee reverses aging").
  2. They must write an article employing pseudo-scientific jargon, fabricated expert quotes, and emotional manipulation to make it sound real.
  3. Groups swap articles. They must read the opposing group's text and write a point-by-point debunking, exposing the linguistic tricks used.
This is the ultimate test of media literacy and linguistic manipulation. Discuss how 'truthiness' is created through specific sentence structures.
5

The Cold Case

Goal: Synthesize ambiguous data.

Solving a complex logic puzzle relying entirely on ambiguous witness testimonies and conflicting historical reports.

Read more
A comprehensive dossier of conflicting evidence, letters, and police reports.
  1. Provide a dense file containing various text types (a diary entry, a lab report, a formal police statement) relating to an unsolved mystery.
  2. None of the texts give the direct answer; the clues are buried in the nuances of the language (e.g., a witness using the past perfect suggests they knew something before it happened).
  3. Groups must synthesize the data into a final, formal indictment.
This demands exhaustive reading comprehension and the ability to track timelines through advanced verb tenses across multiple documents.
6

The Board of Directors

Goal: Dominate corporate discourse.

Leading a hostile corporate takeover meeting where every participant has conflicting secret agendas.

Read more
Extensive character briefs with hidden motives and financial leverage.
  1. Set up a boardroom. Assign roles: CEO, CFO, Activist Investor, Union Rep.
  2. Give each a secret objective (e.g., the CFO is trying to tank the deal to save their own job).
  3. They must conduct the meeting using C2 business English.
  4. They must use interruption strategies, hedging, and subtle intimidation to steer the outcome without revealing their secret motive.
Assess their use of pragmatic turn-taking. At C2, you don't raise your hand to speak; you use language to seamlessly hijack the floor ("If I might interject for a brief moment...").
7

Academic Journal Submission

Goal: Produce publishable writing.

Co-authoring a peer-reviewed style paper complete with citations, intense nominalization, and academic hedging.

Read more
Laptops, access to an academic database (optional).
  1. Groups must write the abstract and introduction of a research paper on a highly complex sociological issue.
  2. They must rely heavily on nominalization (turning verbs into nouns: "They decided to..." becomes "The decision to...").
  3. They must employ academic hedging ("The data *suggests* a *potential* correlation...").
  4. Submit to the 'Editorial Board' (the teacher) for brutal review.
C2 academic writing removes the author. Teach them to eliminate "I think" and replace it with passive constructions ("It is widely posited that...").
8

The Publisher's Desk

Goal: Elevate authorial voice.

Brutally editing peers' essays not just for grammar, but for flow, cadence, and sophisticated authorial voice.

Read more
Draft essays from the students.
  1. Students swap advanced essays. They must act as senior editors at a publishing house.
  2. They do not look for basic grammar; they look for stylistic weakness (cliché phrases, repetitive sentence lengths, weak verbs).
  3. They must rewrite one entire paragraph of their peer's essay to "elevate" it to a publishable standard.
  4. They defend their edits to the original author.
This teaches them that there are many "correct" ways to write a sentence in English, but only one "best" way for a specific context.

Listening Games

Conquer thick dialects, overlapping speech, extreme speed, and deep cultural references.

1

The Polymath's Lecture

Goal: Transcribe technical jargon in dialect.

Taking notes on a highly technical subject delivered by a speaker with a strong, unfamiliar regional accent.

Read more
Audio clips of lectures in Scottish, Irish, or deep Southern US accents.
  1. Play a 3-minute academic lecture (e.g., Astrophysics) delivered by a speaker with a thick Glaswegian or Texan accent.
  2. Students must filter through the unfamiliar phonetics to extract the complex academic data.
  3. They must write a summary of the theory presented.
C2 speakers do not just understand "BBC English." They must demonstrate the phonological flexibility to tune into regional variations rapidly.
2

Micro-Expression Mastery

Goal: Analyze overlapping subtext.

Identifying sarcasm, passive-aggression, and reluctance in overlapping, rapid-fire, multi-speaker dialogue.

Read more
Clips from dense, dialogue-heavy TV shows (e.g., Succession, The West Wing).
  1. Play a scene where 3-4 characters are arguing or talking over each other.
  2. Pause the clip. Ask: "Who currently holds the power in this room, and how do you know based purely on their intonation?"
  3. Students analyze how sighs, interruptions, and pitch changes reveal alliances and animosities.
Authentic English is rarely polite turn-taking. This trains them to isolate crucial psychological data from a wall of chaotic noise.
3

The Chaotic Press Conference

Goal: Extract facts from noise.

Extracting specific facts from an audio clip where multiple journalists are shouting over each other.

Read more
Audio of a hectic White House or Downing Street press briefing.
  1. Provide a worksheet with highly specific questions regarding numbers, dates, or policy names.
  2. Play the audio of a press briefing where the audio quality is imperfect and people are shouting.
  3. Students must tune out the aggressive questions and isolate the specific answers provided by the spokesperson.
This replicates the cognitive load of a real-world C2 environment. Teach them to listen for the spokesperson's "anchor" words ("Let me be clear...", "The numbers are...") to find the data.
4

Simultaneous Paraphrasing

Goal: Real-time translation of ideas.

Listening to a 2-minute political rant and immediately delivering a 30-second perfectly summarized, objective brief.

Read more
An audio clip of an impassioned, rambling speech or rant.
  1. Play a 2-minute audio of someone speaking passionately but repetitively.
  2. As soon as the audio stops, point to a student. They have exactly 30 seconds to strip away the emotion and deliver the core thesis in formal, objective English.
  3. Example: A 2-minute rant about traffic becomes "The speaker argues that municipal infrastructure is severely underfunded."
This is akin to high-level diplomatic interpreting. They must filter out the 'noise' of emotion and capture the pure semantic intent.
5

Decoding Dialects

Goal: Conquer extreme phonetic variation.

Transcribing 10-second clips of thick regional accents (e.g., Scouse, Geordie, Appalachian) word-for-word.

Read more
Short video clips of heavy regional dialects.
  1. Play a 10-second clip of a native speaker with an extreme dialect.
  2. Play it three times. Students must write down standard English words for the heavily accented phonemes they hear.
  3. Review the transcript. Discuss how vowel shifts and dropped consonants define different global Englishes.
This is the ultimate test of phonetic flexibility. Remind them not to focus on spelling, but on matching the sound to context to guess the word.
6

The Filibuster

Goal: Track sprawling syntax.

Tracking an argument where the speaker uses massive digressions, tangents, and complex dependent clauses.

Read more
Audio of a politician filibustering or a philosopher going on a tangent.
  1. Play an audio clip of a single sentence that lasts 45 seconds due to endless relative clauses and tangents.
  2. Students must identify the original subject and the final main verb of the sentence, ignoring all the 'filler' clauses in between.
  3. They must rewrite the 45-second sentence into one simple, 5-word sentence.
This trains working memory. C2 listeners can hold the subject of a sentence in their head for a full minute while waiting for the speaker to finally hit the verb.
7

Cultural Allusion Hunt

Goal: Understand unspoken references.

Identifying and explaining references to Shakespeare, historical events, or pop culture embedded in spontaneous speech.

Read more
Clips of stand-up comedy or late-night talk show interviews.
  1. Play a clip where a speaker makes a quick cultural joke or allusion (e.g., "He's acting like a regular Benedict Arnold").
  2. Pause the audio. Ask: "Who is Benedict Arnold, and what does this imply about the person's character?"
  3. Students must explain the cultural reference and how it shapes the meaning of the conversation.
Language cannot be separated from culture at the C2 level. If they miss the reference, they miss the entire point of the interaction.
8

The Air Traffic Controller

Goal: Process rapid technical data.

Following rapid, highly technical spatial and numerical instructions in a high-stress audio simulation.

Read more
Simulated ATC (Air Traffic Control) audio or emergency dispatch audio, grid paper.
  1. Give students a blank grid.
  2. Play a rapid-fire audio clip full of coordinates, alphanumeric codes, and vector instructions (e.g., "Flight Delta 7 niner, descend to flight level two zero, maintain heading zero four five").
  3. Students must rapidly map the movements or write down the exact sequence of technical data without panicking.
This is the ultimate test of numerical and spatial English under pressure. It proves they process English numbers and letters as fast as their mother tongue.
